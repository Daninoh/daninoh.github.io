reading thoughts...


I always felt that there was something missing in my academic experiences learning about education and educational technologies. In his paper, Moulton describes the challenges that exist in the marriage between existing research and the practice of education. At first, I was reminded of Koedinger, Booth, & Klahr (2013) where they emphasize the complexities of learning science and develop a list of learning principles, but the more I read, the more I realized the focus was on finding the gaps in the bridge between practice and theory. It was refreshing to read something written about learning principles that isn’t obscure and verbose and really getting down to the essential (and extremely blunt) descriptions of the findings.


Beyond the summaries of the existing research, I found the Myths and Misconceptions section to be the most evocative. Namely that (1) “there is no evidence that teachers should design instruction based on students’ learning styles,” (2) “the concept of ‘far transfer’, or the doctrine of formal discipline—is highly suspect,” (3) “with some exceptions for elementary and special education, neuroscience adds very little value to our practical understanding of learning and teaching,” and (4) “as indirect measures of learning, student ratings of instruction are neither worthless nor particularly useful.”


Furthermore, I felt validated by the open questions and gaps Moulton points out, because many of these were the same questions I’ve been thinking about. These questions spanned from thinking about how we assess learners in different contexts to how we integrate evidence-based practices in classrooms and beyond to the trade-offs of having crammable lecture videos. All in all, I thought this paper provided a fantastic summary of known principles as well as a clear framework of potential research questions for educational researchers, educators, and learning designers.

I was a bit curious about the differences between IDEO/Stanford d.school’s design process and the 5D Framework. architecture & essential questions


I studied architecture for my undergraduate degree, and recently I was talking to one of my undergraduate friends about what was wrong with architectural pedagogy. I think we came to an understanding that our education never questioned, "why do we need to study architecture? What is the point of architecture for the researchlic, beyond "creating beautiful experiences? What can architecture do?" We can learn about the environmental and social effects of architecture, but without the underpinning framing of what architecture does for people, there becomes a focus on the built environment, rather than the people we design for.

meeting reflection and summary


Over the past week, I have been thinking about the scope and frame of this project. With a very loose guideline and understanding of the ARTA initiative, I answered four key questions to help set our design problem: (1) Who are my learners?, (2) What do I want learners to experience and do?, (3) What are some inspirational models and precedents we can look at?, and (4) Who do we need to reach out to for more guidance and what else do I need to know?


(1) Who are my learners?
If we can turn this into a mandatory training program, I would like to create a project that teaches all faculty at HGSE. However, if that is out of the scope, I am also interested in creating a platform for faculty who either get referred to these resources or are interested in learning more about the ARTA framework.


(2) What do I want learners to experience and do?
I’m hoping that we can tackle the process of learning to unlearn. With this specific project, I want to see if we can design a tool that informs faculty in their interpersonal communities and classrooms. Perhaps this includes interactive training? In the end, I want to create a platform that the faculty at HGSE will not be overwhelmed with, but also transformative to their thinking, teaching, and curriculum.


(3) What are some inspirational models and precedents we can look at?
I thought about it in three different scopes, what’s not working, what looks effective, and what looks interesting. A few resource collections I have found did not work due to the sheer amount of text and hyperlinks on a single page (EduCause, Brown University). However, resources with a focus on hierarchy or an innovative solution through a dedicated curriculum seemed like more effective methods of teaching faculty. Lastly, I found a VR project called 1,000 Cut Journey from Columbia University that seemed very interesting. I am interested in seeing if there are more anti-racist initiatives that are as interactive and engaging as this!


(4) Who do we need to reach out to for more guidance and what else do I need to know?
I would love some more precedent models and projects to work off of. If we knew of any interesting media projects like the 1,000 Cut Journey, that would be phenomenal (riffing off of Bernard’s comment, maybe a chatbot already exists as a precedent?).

I would also love to hold a conversation with any faculty who is currently working in DEIB, right now (the first people that come to mind are Dr. Josephine Kim and Dr. Gretchen Brion-Meisels, but I’m open to anyone!). I want a better framework to work with, instead of trying to create something blindly. What are some issues faculty are seeing with current DEIB initiatives? What resources are people exposed to and what are their means of access?


Currently, Bern and I are beginning to think about a collaborative project, incorporating some of the features of the precedent project we have looked into – in order to create an engaging, interactive platform with reduced cognitive load.


nuance in design


This week had a lot of ‘big idea’ overlap between all of my courses and work. Specifically, in between thinking about designing spaces and objects for disabled folks (with Sara Hendren in her amazing course, Investigating Normal), to designing for children (with Mitch Resnick in Learning Creative Learning and my teaching position with Bertrand Schneider), to designing for learners–there was a floating term that seemed to encapsulate my week, nuance.


In response to my question to last week, where I asked if there is a difference between the 5D ‘waterfall’ flow and the IDEO design thinking ‘recipe,’ while it isn’t quite highlighted in the actual simplified depictions, the differences lies in the nuances between designing for a general user base and designing for specific learners.


As designers, we have to be aware of the unique affordances of each of our clients and users. Just as the needs and pain points of disabled individuals are different from children, we have to be aware of the nuances between each individual within that subcategory. While design thinking often aims to create a solution through a hypothetical persona generated from a selected group of people, I challenge this idea and imagine a design process that incorporates an individualized solution through co-design. One amazing precedent we discussed is the ADA (Adaptive Design Association), where they design furniture and other artifacts through one-on-one, personal customization with affordable materials. This personal relationship between the designer and user results in a product where the actual end user is a part of the ideation process. I begin to wonder if I could think about incorporating this into the ARTA initiative…

equity and CBEs


I'm absolutely conflicted on the cost benefit of Competency Based Education in terms of equity. While I agree that CBEs enable individuals who aren't able to pursue a 'traditional' education path to both learn and get credentials, it is undeniable that many employers and professionals put value into institutional prestige. When we think of CBEs in the bubble of education and learning, separated from industry, CBEs are certainly effective in engaging individuals with their specific educational needs. However, reality shows that there are industry leaders, professionals, and employers who value their own perception of quality over qualification. Equitable education is about more than just having access to credentials and platforms for learning -- it should be able to provide the same levels of opportunities. But I see this as not an issue with CBEs, but a systemic issue.


On a different note, I find that in fields where competency can be proven with supplementary evidence, CBEs are much more effective. That is, in fields like design and software development, students leave with both a credential, showing competency as evaluated by an external institute or person, and a complementary portfolio that a potential employer can evaluate themselves. Companies and employers who are recognized as industry leaders are employing more and more professionals without degrees, due to the rising enrollment in Bootcamps. While I understand that CBEs and Bootcamps are not necessarily the same, perhaps the production of external portfolios, beyond the fields of design and computer science, may benefit in exhibiting the competencies of those who complete their studies? I think my biggest issue with credentials and accreditation is that it sets up a system where an employer relies on a third party to evaluate the competencies of an individual. In this vein, I think CBEs are generally seen as a credential of less quality, due to how much this employer trusts or values this third party, rather than the individual. Perhaps there's a way we can think about integrating demonstrable skills into every industry -- in the form of nicely packed case studies or inquiry into previously completed projects.


While I was writing up my response all I could think about was... why do we need credentials? In my perspective, credentials are for corporations with capitalist needs who need a way to discern the ability of a prospective employee (which I am clearly and unfortunately subscribed to, per my decision to join this program). In a disability design course, I recently read a paper called These Pushful Days by Douglas Baynton, which talked about concepts such as crip time and how modern industry has created an environment where many inequities, especially for persons with disabilities, are formed by the conceptualization of life as a race. With this in mind, I wonder what industries might look like if employers took the time to get to know someone's capabilities beyond one to two interviews, rather than relying on a quick scan of credentials, which shows competency as evaluated by an external entity. I think this is why ePortfolios might be a great tool in all industries. With ePortfolios or any medium in which an individual can wrap up previous experiences or competencies in a digestible format, there might be less reliance on credentials, and as Julia Kim put it -- get closer to educational liberation!

reflections on MOOCs and more


I enrolled in four courses in the edX platform: Introduction to Haptics from StanfordX, Introduction to Mechatronics from GeorgiaTechX, TinyML from HarvardX, and CS50's Introduction to Game Development from HarvardX.


The first three courses, on haptics, mechatronics, and TinyML, all required external toolkits which students would have to purchase (ranging from $50 - $250), which is a limitation of the course topic due to the physical requirements of learning about robots and microcontrollers. Due to my personal interest in these topics (intrinsic motivation), the lecture videos from the Haptics, Mechatronics, and TinyML were all fascinating (especially the first two). The production quality and depth of the Haptics course, integrating application, personal at home/at desk experiments, and a personal touch (through digital sketching from the lecturer over a presentation) helped me feel like I was in a synchronous lecture. While the lecturer's face was not visible for a majority of the lecture, the digital hand sketching in the presentation helped me feel like I was actually listening to a lecturer. The Mechatronics course was similar in the high quality of the videos. However, these videos felt far more rehearsed and formal (which I personally did not enjoy as much). On the other hand, while the quality of the course videos and course materials for both of the HarvardX courses were fantastic, I quickly gave up on both due to the sheer amount of time it took for me to read a chapter for the TinyML course [and also the daunting 8-10 different sections in each module] and watch to a 1.5 hour long video for the CS50 course [which I might finish when I have more time, after April].


As I am not personally interested in credentials and getting verified certificates (and even more so paying for it), I was not thrilled by the courses with longer quizzes. The Mechatronics course did a fantastic job in prompting only a couple (2-3) questions per 5-15 minute lecture video. These lectures would then be supplemented by a lab assignment, which enrolled students could submit.


On the subject of community, I wasn't thrilled by the course format of the CS50 course. However, I did appreciate that the introduction of the course included VARIOUS (instagram, discord, twitter, etc.) social media platforms where students could collect and connect. The Mechatronics course, on the other hand, included two different forums for Q&A, where TAs and the instructor would be active in, and a Get to Know Each Other forum, where only students would (maybe) be active in. Considering that these forums were integrated into the edX platform, I do not foresee many people utilizing it. Whereas, the community platforms that CS50 uses are ubiquitous and easily accessible.


Overall, I loved the courses with short bursts of information, so I could approach each section with less fear and preparation, and also appreciate a less formal lecture environment (after all, in higher-ed institutions, we have professors who don't memorize a certain script and explain things as they go).

I am ready to begin creating the visual design [wireframes & more]! I’ve taken the past week to synthesize our many findings from talks and organize my thoughts from last week (theories of andragogy, elements needed in this platform, etc). While I am a little bit behind schedule, where I would have ideally done lo-fi prototypes by now, I am glad that we took a bit more time to think about all our end users, and what this platform might signify for the future of the ARTA initiative.


In terms of end users, I’ve organized the current website framework by paddlers, swimmers, and divers. This is a technique I learned while interning at a natural history museum, where exhibit designers need to create exhibits that are curated to those who will simply gaze and walk past an exhibit, those who will stand and read some of the descriptions, and those who will really engage with an exhibit. As noted in my last reflection, this platform aims, both in the framing of paddler, swimmers, and divers and in the frame of adult learning, to provide multiple ways of engaging with anti-racist resources. The Instructional Moves Pathways are foundational for how this platform would work, focusing on pathways that feature videos with curated resources (and summaries), followed by applied scenarios (in video form, just like IM), and a short self-reflection at the end of these videos. Furthermore, taking input from Bill, I have implemented a community section to this platform.

<<   reflection week 13

capstone progress


This week, I began developing my hi-fidelity prototype for the ARTA resources and presented my work to the ISD team (Josh, Ivonne, and Allison). The hi-fidelity prototype uses the paddler, swimmer, diver framework to organize its contents. For example, a faculty member may only have the capacity to browse through the initial list of competencies. In the homepage, they are able to scroll through the list of competencies with a brief summary of each. If a faculty member is feeling a bit more adventurous, they are able to click on a competency and read through some of the strategies they might approach to achieve the competency. If the faculty member really wants to dive into actionable steps and learn more about existing resources, they will be able to click on a strategy which will lead to a pop-up of related resources.




The resources page also takes into consideration a personalization feature, where a learner is able to filter the colossal list of resources available and save specific, standout resources to their homepages. I was imagining that this would be done either through browser cookies or a HarvardKey account.




From my meeting with the ISD team, I learned a lot more about existing communities of practice on anti-racism at HGSE. Specifically, Josh listed (1) Conversations on Race and Racism, (2) the Faculty of Color group, and (3) Developing as Anti-Racist White Educators. Perhaps the community page on the ARTA resource page could be a platform of conversation for these communities–either guiding newer faculty to these existing communities or helping facilitate conversations within these communities (I’m more inclined to pursue the second).


As this semester wraps up and the gallery walk approaches, I will be finishing up the hi-fidelity prototype (the about page), adding more interactions, and organizing my capstone into a two minute elevator pitch. I plan on framing everything around the paddler, swimmer, diver framework and discussing some of my larger design decisions (i.e. reason for the inclusion of specific pages).

final reflection


The past several weeks in this course have been a wonderful learning experience. I really appreciate the organization of the course, especially providing us time to just learn about learning and designing in the beginning and providing us plenty of support and time to wrap up our newfound understanding in our capstones. While I had no initial desire to learn about MOOCs and Open Learning, as I had some exposure to it in undergrad and wasn’t too interested, I’m thankful that this course helped me critique/appreciate it and see it in a different lens.


I would also say that, due to the remote nature of the course, and the slightly disjointed communities created by the different project teams, I wasn’t able to feel the same level of community in this course as my other courses. While I completely understand that the class being remote limits certain aspects, I wish there were more opportunities for me to connect with my peers throughout this course. Considering that there were a couple of people pursuing completely different projects through the Teaching and Learning Lab, it would have been fantastic to be able to connect with people beyond my smaller team – both in terms of getting feedback and receiving different perspectives (after all, interdisciplinary work is a phenomenal way of working).


Beyond this social aspect, which might just be the case for me, I’m thankful for the breadth of knowledge and concepts we covered in this course and I’m excited to take everything forward into my future work! Working with the ARTA (special thanks to Katherine!) and Instructional Moves team was a fantastic opportunity, especially in terms of understanding what learning designers do and how the curation of learning material happens. I am glad that I was able to be a part of a team pursuing such a challenging initiative, and I hope that my work will be beneficial to the future work of the team!
